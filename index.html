Elliot Gavin Keenan

Myself In My Own Words

Each step has a certain tension; the premeditated rhythm of his pacing evokes the mechanical motions of a Newton's cradle. Click... click. Yet there is a degree of self-consciousness about his graceless stride -- perhaps a consequence of being made aware of the awkwardness of his own movements from an early age. He is about five foot, with a compact physique. The impression he gives is androgynous if not for the dark stubble that traces his jaw. His hair -- thick, coarse curls the color of burnt charcoal - grazes the top of his round spectacles. He wears idiosyncracy like a favorite t-shirt.

He doesn't quite look at you as he speaks. Nonetheless, with his gaze meandering about the room, he is quietly observant. He has an intelligent demeanor. When he speaks, his voice has a certain effervescence to it - a breathy timbre that somehow complements his careful articulation of speech. (Although he cannot sing, he believes his vocal range to be a lyric tenor.) His conversational speech flows in a metrical rhythm, one reminiscient of poetry readings thanks to its highly intentional and somewhat monotonous inflection. Yet, when he reads poetry aloud, his vocal prosody takes on a strikingly rhapsodic quality.

About Elliot

Elliot Gavin Keenan is 23 years old and a PhD student in Human Development & Psychology at UCLA. He is interested in creating statistical models of mental health conditions. His hobbies include playing strategy board games, anything related to Pokémon, and painting.

Everything Else



Read
Published in Gravel in February 2017, this creative nonfiction essay "speaks of beautiful thinking".
She’s a doctoral student in the psychology department. But she told me that she used to write as if seized by a certain fervor for it, for the language, for poetry. I imagined Van Gogh and his passion for painting, his insatiable hunger. I thought I wanted to kiss those lips stained with yellow paint. Yellow, the color of the edges of a street, the boundaries of a self crossed like two neurons, the actualization of a synesthetic dream. To imbibe it is to take all of that in, the passion, life thrust under your tongue. I wanted that.


Notes to Self



Read
Published in Lunch Ticket in Spring/Summer 2018.
Yes, one day — maybe soon, I will put down the pen, and I will leave you. Maybe ECT will bring me back for a while. But not forever. When this happens, you will be hurt and alone, I know. When I cease to exist within our mind, I suppose that is like dying. I have always been characterized as a confident voice; yet I am face-to-face with an indescribable fear — I do not know what will happen to me after I die. But, I think I will miss being alive; breathing, eating, writing, living, even if you do not remember me or anything anymore, maybe in some way, somewhere — I will live on in you.
